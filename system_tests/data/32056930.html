Pregnancy exposure to venlafaxine—Therapeutic drug monitoring in maternal blood, amniotic fluid and umbilical cord blood and obstetrical outcomes

Abstract

Background

For treatment with psychotropic drugs during pregnancy, extended therapeutic drug monitoring is recommended for individual therapy adjustment. We measured venlafaxine (VEN), O-desmethylvenlafaxine (ODV) and active moiety, AM (sum of VEN+ODV) concentrations in maternal serum, amniotic fluid and umbilical cord blood.

Methods

Concentrations of VEN, ODVEN and AM were measured in nine mother-infant pairs at time of delivery; in five cases, amniotic fluid samples were available. Concentrations are reported as median values, first (Q1) and third (Q3) quartiles and ranges. Penetration ratio was calculated by dividing concentrations of VEN, ODVEN and AM in amniotic fluid and umbilical cord blood by maternal serum concentrations.

Results

Median daily dosage of venlafaxine was 75 mg (range 37.5–225 mg). There were no significant correlations between daily dose, maternal serum, umbilical cord blood and amniotic fluid concentrations. Median penetration ratio into amniotic fluid was 2.5 (range 0.56–4.48). Median penetration ratio into fetal circulation was 1.05 (range 0.62–2.08). Median concentration of AM was 223.8 ng/mL, range 33.9–338.0 ng/mL (maternal serum), 789.0 ng/mL, range 309-1052.5 ng/mL (amniotic fluid) and 291.0 ng/mL, range 21.1–448.4 ng/mL (cord blood).

Discussion

VEN, ODVEN and AM concentrations in maternal serum, amniotic fluid and umbilical cord blood indicate that the fetus might have been exposed to relatively high concentrations throughout pregnancy. High concentrations in amniotic fluid indicate an increased penetration into and/or accumulation within amniotic fluid and a decreased elimination out of amniotic fluid. Findings indicate that fetal in-utero exposition to venlafaxine is higher compared to other antidepressants.

1. Introduction

Antidepressants are increasingly prescribed during pregnancy (Jimenez-Solem, 2014; Kallen et al., 2013; Thomas and Yates, 2012). Data suggest that, after weighing the risks of untreated maternal depression for both, mother and unborn child, the use of antidepressants is considered favorable (Muzik and Hamilton, 2016). Medical professionals in psychiatry and obstetrics share the concerns about potential negative outcomes in terms of congenital malformations or adverse effects due to antidepressant treatment during pregnancy.
Venlafaxine (VEN) is a selective serotonin- and norepinephrine-reuptake inhibitor (SSNRI) used to treat depression and anxiety. VEN is metabolized via distinct CYP isoenzymes, mainly by CYP2D6 but also by CYP2C19 to O-desmethylvenlafaxine, ODVEN, its major active metabolite, but also by CYP3A4 and by CYP2C19 to its inactive metabolite N-desmethylvenlafaxine (NDVEN). The concentration of VEN + ODVEN (active moiety, AM) is considered as the clinically most relevant parameter (Hiemke et al., 2018). Due to an increased CYP2D6 activity during pregnancy, the metabolism of VEN to ODVEN may be increased, however, the general suggestion is to keep venlafaxine doses stable throughout pregnancy (Westin et al., 2017). In a small study sample of breastfeeding mothers, VEN and ODVEN were found in breast milk and daily dosage of the drug correlated positively with the concentration of the active moiety in breast milk (Schoretsanitis et al., 2018). The manufacturer of venlafaxine recommends that venlafaxine should be prescribed in pregnancy only if mandatory after careful risk evaluation because there are no well-controlled studies that adequately assess the risks for pregnant women and their exposed infants.
Venlafaxine has been linked to an increased risk of pre-eclampsia in the second and third trimester (Palmsten et al., 2013) but recent studies do not suggest teratogenic effects or an increased risk of congenital malformations (Einarson et al., 2001; Furu et al., 2015; Lassen et al., 2016). While selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors have been linked to a higher risk of persistent pulmonary hypertension (PPHN) in newborns, the risk of venlafaxine as a selective serotonin and norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor (SSNRI) remains unclear due to limited data (Berard et al., 2017). Transient behavioral abnormalities such as irritability, agitation or hypotonia in neonates and even to a higher degree in premature infants have been reported as increased in infants that were exposed to venlafaxine during pregnancy (Ferreira et al., 2007). These symptoms have been described as part of a neonatal discontinuation syndrome while no long-term effects have been reported so far (Holland and Brown, 2017).
Untreated maternal depression poses its own particular risks, as depression is associated with a higher risk for postpartum depression and long-term child psychopathology (Nulman et al., 2012). Maternal depression has been linked to increased cortisol and norepinephrine levels as well as to increased admissions to neonatal intensive care units (Gentile, 2017), growth retardation and lower birth weight (Davalos et al., 2012). Discontinuation of an ongoing psychopharmacological treatment or untreated mental illness may lead to relapse and creates risks for maternal suicidal behavior (Klys et al., 2007; Paulzen et al., 2015b), data underlined by a large British study showing that untreated depression in the perinatal period is linked to increased suicides (Khalifeh et al., 2016).
The main barrier between fetal and maternal circulation is the placenta acting as a structural barrier that consists of active membrane transporters and metabolizing enzymes of the cytochrome P450 family to prevent harmful compounds from entering fetal circulation (Ganapathy et al., 2000; Prouillac and Lecoeur, 2010). A variety of mechanisms in the human placenta such as p-glycoprotein (P-gp) and breast cancer resistance protein (BCRP) protects the fetus via active efflux but the expression of these mechanisms changes during pregnancy (Iqbal et al., 2012). The transfer of pharmacological compounds via the placenta into fetal circulation is depending on its physiochemical properties. Factors that influence placental transfer are blood pH, molecular weight of a drug, lipid solubility, protein binding in maternal and fetal circulation, availability of drug transport proteins in the placenta as well as binding affinities to placental tissue (Hutson et al., 2011).
Therapeutic drug monitoring in maternal serum, amniotic fluid and umbilical cord blood allows to quantify the extent of placental transfer of a drug into fetal circulation or its accumulation in amniotic fluid. The ability to measure fetal exposure to a drug allows professionals to choose drugs that guarantee a low penetration into fetal circulation. Quantification of a drug in compartments of fetal circulation also allows to assess whether neonatal complications associated with fetal exposure may be causally related.
Quantifying the extent of placental passage or knowledge about the accumulation of a drug in amniotic fluid can facilitate drug selection and ultimately provide insight into whether or not neonatal complications are directly related to drug exposure with measurable drug concentrations in amniotic fluid or fetal circulation (Paulzen et al., 2018; Paulzen et al., 2017b; Paulzen et al., 2019).
As data on fetal exposure with venlafaxine during pregnancy are missing so far, aim of this study was to analyze the distribution of venlafaxine and its metabolite in maternal serum, amniotic fluid and umbilical cord blood at the time of delivery. We aimed to unravel pharmacokinetic patterns by measuring drug concentrations in these three compartments. We further sought to account for the relation between the applied daily dose of venlafaxine and the maternal serum as well as the amniotic fluid and umbilical cord blood concentrations under naturalistic/clinical conditions.

2. Materials and methods

This investigation is part of an ongoing observational study on therapeutic drug monitoring of the distribution pattern of different psychotropic drugs during pregnancy. Drug concentrations in maternal blood, amniotic fluid and umbilical cord blood were determined at the time of delivery. Distribution patterns of different psychotropic drugs have been published recently (Paulzen et al., 2018; Paulzen et al., 2017a; Paulzen et al., 2017b; Paulzen et al., 2015c; Paulzen et al., 2019). TDM is carried out as a collaboration between the Department of Psychiatry, Psychotherapy, and Psychosomatics, and the Department of Gynecology and Obstetrics at the University Hospital of RWTH Aachen University, Germany, since November 2012. The local Ethics Committee approved the study protocol as an observational study and all participants gave written informed consent.
Data of nine pregnant women, age ranging from 28 to 40 years (mean 33 ± 4; median 32), and nine newborns are presented. Women received venlafaxine extended release formulation throughout their pregnancies in daily doses between 37.5 mg and 225 mg and were in stable clinical conditions at the time of delivery. All patients received venlafaxine once per day in the morning. None of the pregnant women suffered from an acute exacerbation of the psychiatric disease throughout their pregnancies. Last dose adaptions were performed at least two weeks before delivery, so steady state conditions could be assumed at the time of delivery. One patient received a stable co-medication with 7.5 mg mirtazapine per day, one was co-medicated with 100 ug levothyroxine due to hypothyroidism and one patient received 23.75 mg metoprolol and 250 mg of methyldopa t.i.d. due to hypertension.
Eight patients were diagnosed with major depressive disorder, one with obsessive-compulsive disorder. Two women also suffered from comorbid obsessive-compulsive disorder and panic disorder. All nine mother-infant pairs provided maternal serum and umbilical cord blood at delivery. In five cases, amniotic fluid was also available (see Table 1) while in four cases, amniotic fluid could not be gathered due to rupture of membranes during labor or section.

3. Quantification of venlafaxine and O-desmethylvenlafaxine

Maternal blood, umbilical cord blood and amniotic fluid samples were taken simultaneously at the time of delivery under steady-state conditions with regard to the ingested drug but due to clinical circumstances not as trough levels. As indicator for drug concentrations in maternal blood, we used serum concentrations. Serum, cord blood and amniotic fluid were prepared by centrifugation at 14,171 g for 15 min. VEN and ODVEN concentrations in maternal serum, amniotic fluid and umbilical cord blood were determined by UPLC. Chromatographic separation was conducted with a Waters Acquity® UPLC system with gradient elution on a Waters Acquity® UPLC BEH-C18 column (2.1 mm × 50 mm, 1.7-μm particle size). For quantification, a Waters Acquity® TQ detector was used. The method is linear from the designated limit of quantification of 4.0 ng/mL up to the upper limit of 504 ng/mL for VEN and 3.0 ng/mL up to the upper limit of 721 ng/mL for ODV. Intra- and inter-assay precision across four quality control levels were ≤4.4% and ≤5.7% for VEN and ≤4.2% and ≤6.6% for ODVEN, respectively. Concentrations below the limit of quantification (4.0 ng/mL) were included in the analysis as half the values of the lower limit of quantification (e.g. 2.0 ng/mL in the case of a result of <4.0 ng/mL for venlafaxine). This method was applied in two cases of maternal serum and two cases of cord blood concentrations that showed VEN concentrations below the lower limit of quantification.

4. Statistical analysis

As a primary outcome, we defined the active moiety (AM) as the concentration of VEN + ODVEN, which is generally considered as the clinically most relevant parameter (Hiemke et al., 2018). We provide single values for VEN and ODVEN in Table 2. To account for the range of daily doses of venlafaxine received by the patients, we divided the serum-, amniotic fluid- and cord blood-concentrations of the active moiety by the daily dose of venlafaxine (applied once daily), resulting in a concentration-by-dose-ratio (C/D) for AM in maternal serum (MS), amniotic fluid (AF) and cord blood (CB), respectively. Data are provided as median values, first (Q1) and third quartiles (Q3) as well as ranges between min and max values. Additionally, the penetration ratio into amniotic fluid (for five patients) and cord blood of AM was calculated. For this purpose, the concentration of AM in AF as well as CB was divided by its counterpart value in MS, reflecting the penetration-ratio into amniotic fluid and cord blood, respectively. Correlations were computed to assess the relation between the drug concentrations in maternal serum, amniotic fluid and cord blood, as well as the relation of these concentrations with the daily dose of venlafaxine. Due to the small sample size, non-parametric tests (Spearman's correlation) were applied and statistical analysis was carried out using IBM SPSS Statistics version 24.0 (SPSS Inc. Chicago, USA).

5. Results

Data on daily doses, serum-, amniotic fluid- and cord blood-concentrations of venlafaxine, concentration-to-dose and penetration ratios and data on obstetrical outcomes are displayed in Table 1, Table 2, Table 3. Biological and anamnestic data of the pregnant women is displayed in Table 1. Body mass index (BMI) was available for eight of the nine women but for four women weight measurement were not performed at time of delivery (last weight measurement for patient 2 one month prior, patient 5 three weeks prior, patient 6 nine days prior, patient 7 ten days prior) resulting in under-estimation of BMI in these four cases. Blood tests at the time of delivery showed no renal impairment in any of the patients. Patient 3 had slightly elevated liver enzymes at the time of delivery (AST 56 U/l, ALT 65 U/l, laboratory reference range <35) with the remaining eight patients showing no elevated liver enzymes. Patient 3 was the only patient with protein levels within the reference range, the other eight patients all showed slightly reduced blood proteins (range: 5.6 – 6.3 g/dl, laboratory reference range 6.6 – 8.7 g/dl). Only one pregnant patient (# 2) was a smoker. There was no indication of illicit drug use or alcohol consumption in any of the pregnant women. Compliance with psychopharmacologic treatment can be considered high as all women received outpatient treatment and maternal serum concentrations indicated compliance with antidepressant therapy. Time of last intake of venlafaxine was not exactly measured but had to be estimated. As all patients took venlafaxine in the morning we assumed intake at 8 a.m to calculate the hours between taking of samples and time of last dose of venlafaxine. Obstetrical outcome data were available for all nine infants. Three newborns were temporarily admitted to the intensive care unit, but did not need any further therapy; in two cases, color and consistency of amniotic fluid indicated a passage of meconium into the amniotic fluid. A low APGAR score (Appearance (skin color), Pulse (heart rate), Grimace (reflex irritability), Activity (muscle tone), and Respiration) without further complications was reported in these three cases. Regarding body weight, eight infants were within a range between the 5th and the 95th percentile and one showed increased body weight for age and was at the 99th percentile. The median daily dosage of venlafaxine was 75 mg (Q1: 75 mg, Q3: 150 mg, range 37.5–225 mg).

6. Discussion

Data on fetal exposure in pregnant women taking venlafaxine is scarce, the general recommendation is that the use in pregnancy is possible after careful evaluation of the risks for both, mother and unborn child (Nielsen and Damkier, 2012). In women medicated with an SSNRI during pregnancy, the administration of venlafaxine is favored over duloxetine because the latter appears to increase the miscarriage risk (Vitale et al., 2016). The here presented results are part of an ongoing observational TDM analysis aiming to characterize individual variability in concentrations of drugs given during pregnancy in different biological compartments. Intense TDM during pregnancy and delivery allows to take into account the placental transfer and accumulation properties of different psychotropic drugs given during pregnancy for optimizing the outcome and safety for mother and child. Drug concentrations in amniotic fluid and umbilical cord blood in addition to maternal serum concentrations may serve as an indicator for drug exposure of the infant. Consequently, the employment of therapeutic drug monitoring in psychiatry and obstetrics and specifically in particularly vulnerable patient subgroups such as pregnant women introduces an important clinical tool to provide individualized psychopharmacotherapy thereby ensuring efficacy and safety (Hiemke et al., 2018).
To our knowledge, our study is the first to include TDM data from maternal serum, amniotic fluid and umbilical cord blood concentrations of venlafaxine at the same sampling time. Our data indicate that the process of fetal development occurs in an environment of constant exposure to pharmacologically relevant concentrations of venlafaxine and its active metabolite O-desmethylvenlafaxine. We found that fetal exposure at the time of delivery in all cases occurred with considerably higher concentrations of VEN and ODVEN in amniotic fluid, in one case almost fivefold, compared to maternal serum concentrations. The data suggest that a significant accumulation of venlafaxine may occur in the amniotic fluid, as the placenta acts as a structural and biochemical barrier to venlafaxine only to a limited extent. Regarding cord blood, the fetus is exposed to VEN and ODVEN concentrations that are equivalent or slightly higher than maternal serum concentrations.
Concerning the presence of venlafaxine in amniotic fluid, literature is very limited with only five published cases. One assessed the concentration of venlafaxine in amniotic fluid via amniocentesis in week 17 in a woman taking 100 mg of venlafaxine twice per day at the time of amniocentesis (Hostetter et al., 2000). There, the concentration of venlafaxine was 16 ng/mL and O-desmethylvenlafaxine was found at a concentration of 313 ng/mL. As no maternal serum was obtained, a ratio between maternal serum and amniotic fluid could not be calculated. At the time of delivery at week 36, the patient received 150 mg of venlafaxine twice per day. At that time, maternal serum concentration of 335 ng/mL (VEN) and 97 ng/mL (ODVEN) was found, suggesting that adequate maternal serum concentrations had been be reached during pregnancy. The metabolite-to-parent ratio (ODVEN/VEN) in maternal serum was 0.29 while in amniotic fluid it was 19.56 (VEN 16 ng/mL; ODVEN 313 ng/mL) suggesting an accumulation of the hydrophilic metabolite ODVEN in amniotic fluid. In both twin newborns, the authors found comparably high concentrations of the active moiety and relatively low metabolite-to-parent ratios of 0.52 and 0.59, respectively. The ratio of drug concentrations between umbilical cord blood and maternal serum was 1.7 for venlafaxine and 3.13 for O-desmethylvenlafaxine (Hostetter et al., 2000). Another study of four pregnant women taking venlafaxine showed high transfer rates of venlafaxine across the placenta with mean ratios of 1.73 for venlafaxine and 3.0 for O-desmethylvenlafaxine in amniotic fluid compared to maternal serum (Loughhead et al., 2006). One case showed a ratio between cord blood and maternal serum of 1.1 for venlafaxine and 1.0 for O-desmethylvenlafaxine in a woman taking 150 mg venlafaxine per day (Rampono et al., 2004). Two additional cases of women taking 75 mg venlafaxine per day showed a cord blood to maternal serum ratio of 0.8 and 1.64 for venlafaxine and 0.79 and 0.56 for O-desmethylvenlafaxine (Sit et al., 2011). Another study with eleven mother-infant pairs showed median cord blood to maternal serum ratios of 0.72 for venlafaxine and 1.08 for O-desmethylvenlafaxine (Rampono et al., 2009). Our findings show comparable results of equivalent or slightly higher concentrations of venlafaxine and its active metabolite in fetal cord blood compared to maternal serum.
The here presented data are the first case series with data on amnionic fluid during delivery showing increased penetration rates in all mothers. This suggests that in the case of venlafaxine the placenta does not act as an effective barrier to keep venlafaxine and O-desmethylvenlafaxine out of fetal circulation during pregnancy. The effectiveness of the placental barrier depends on the physiochemical properties of xenobiotics in general (Hutson et al., 2011). Venlafaxine and its active metabolite are characterized by low plasma protein binding, a phenomenon that may explain its high ability to pass the placental barrier. The outstanding ability of VEN and ODVEN to cross barriers was shown in cerebrospinal fluid with very high concentrations of both compounds (Paulzen et al., 2015a). This may explain the high concentrations seen in amniotic fluid, and lead to significant exposure of the fetus because the fetus is constantly exposed to the compounds via ingestion, transcutaneous absorption and respiratory exposure of amniotic fluid, the latter bypassing fetal hepatic metabolism (Underwood et al., 2005). Assuming that the fetus swallows up to 760 ml amniotic fluid per day in late pregnancy, a median active moiety of 789 ng/mL would roughly equal a daily intake of 0.60 mg venlafaxine by the fetus (Loughhead et al., 2006) additionally to the amount of VEN and ODVEN that passes the placental barrier within the blood circulation. This additional intake and the already high blood concentration may explain the occurrence of venlafaxine discontinuation syndromes in newborns (Ansary et al., 2014; de Moor et al., 2003; Holland and Brown, 2017).
Samples were taken during delivery which occurred between an estimated 2.5 and 22 h after last intake of venlafaxine. From a pharmacokinetic point of view, aspects such as maternal peak serum concentration and the different half-lives of VEN and ODVEN may have influenced the results although all women were under steady-state conditions at delivery. Unfortunately, there is no data to form hypothesis on how maternal peak serum concentrations influence concentrations in cord blood and amniotic fluid. Half-life of VEN is 5 ± 2 and of ODVEN 11 ± 2 according to the medication guide provided by the manufacturer and the FDA (Wyeth). A small study in healthy volunteers reported longer half-lives of 11 h for VEN and 14 h for ODVEN with only minor influence of metabolizer status which was not assessed in our study (Preskorn et al., 2009). Taken together, the longer biological half-life of ODVEN compared to VEN may have contributed to higher concentrations in particular as half-lives may be even higher in the fetal hepatic system which is still under development.
Another possible explanation for the accumulation of the active metabolite ODVEN may be found in substantial differences between fetal and adult hepatic metabolism. Fetal hepatic drug metabolism possibly prolongs exposure to metabolites. As they are more water soluble, they do not cross the placenta back into maternal circulation. Furthermore, as these metabolites are excreted renally via fetal urine into amniotic fluid, they are re-ingested (Ring et al., 1999). Hence, the high ratio of ODVEN/VEN may be due to differences in hepatic metabolism in the fetus compared to the adult and not due to a preferential placental passage of the metabolite ODVEN compared to unmetabolized venlafaxine. However, this remains a hypothesis because data on fetal drug elimination is limited.
To our knowledge, our study is the first to assess concentrations of VEN and ODVEN in maternal serum, amniotic fluid and cord blood at the same time of delivery giving profound information on the distribution between the three biological compartments. Venlafaxine seems to differ from other psychotropic drugs. In a recent study of lamotrigine, we found penetration ratios of 0.58 into amniotic fluid (AF) and 0.81 for umbilical cord blood (CB) (Paulzen et al., 2015c). The penetration ratio for the second-generation antipsychotic quetiapine into AF was 0.44 and 0.18 into cord blood (Paulzen et al., 2018). For antidepressants, we found ratios of 0.57 (AF) and 0.36 (CB) for sertraline, and a ratio of 1.8 (AF) and 0.78 (CB) for citalopram (Paulzen et al., 2017a; Paulzen et al., 2017b). Thus, for all psychotropic drugs assessed so far, median penetration ratios into amniotic fluid were below one except citalopram.
In the case of venlafaxine, we found a relatively high penetration into cord blood (median 1.05) and into amniotic fluid (median 2.50). Our findings imply that fetal exposure to venlafaxine occurs with higher concentrations compared to other antidepressants such as citalopram and sertraline. Hence, the option to switch the antidepressant treatment strategy to a drug that shows lower penetration properties into fetal circulation could be discussed as an option with a patient before or during pregnancy. The prediction of amniotic fluid penetration of a given drug may be dependent on pharmacokinetic parameters (such as hydrophilic features, protein binding, active placental transport), but also on the participation of hydrophilic metabolites to the active moiety. Wilson's six principles of teratology state that manifestations of deviant development increase in frequency and degree as dosage (or better: concentrations) increases from no-effect to the totally lethal level (Jelinek, 2005). Our study did not address toxicity itself, but by publishing information on the extent of fetal exposure, future research can be directed to ensure drug safety and efficacy during pregnancy to avoid fetal toxicity. Although the correlation between maternal serum concentrations of AM and cord blood concentrations did not reach significance, it can be assumed that serum concentrations are a much better indicator for fetal exposure than the daily dose of venlafaxine.
Taken together, therapeutic drug monitoring of venlafaxine during pregnancy is a valuable tool to guide antidepressant treatment by addressing the minimal effective serum concentration helping to predict the in-utero exposition of the fetus. Given the high penetration ratio into amniotic fluid which indicates an additional source of drug exposure of the infant, psychiatrists should carefully consider continuing treatment with minimal effective doses or even consider a switch to a different antidepressant drug before or during pregnancy with a lower penetration into fetal circulation.

7. Limitations

The small number of mother-infant pairs and the few samples of amniotic fluid concentrations in our study pose considerable limitations. Our findings do not allow for general conclusions on concentrations that may be expected within the investigated environments in fetal and maternal circulation and related effects. Given that measurements were performed during clinical routine at delivery, drug concentrations do not represent trough levels, therefore limiting the generalizability of the results. Hence, findings do not allow to conclude that comparable drug concentrations are present in the same amount throughout pregnancy. Further information about the long-term effects of antidepressants during pregnancy is mandatory to assess the risk profile of psychotropic drugs that are prescribed in pregnant patients. Inclusion of genetic data such as information on polymorphic cytochrome P450 genes would have enabled the further analysis of the mechanisms mediating fetal drug effects.

  • Antidepressant transfer into amniotic fluid, umbilical cord blood & breast milk: A systematic review & combined analysis

    Data regarding the ability of antidepressants to enter fetal, newborn and infant fluids have become gradually available, but mechanisms of antidepressant transfer remain poorly understood.     We performed a systematic literature search of PubMed and EMBASE to identify studies with concentrations of antidepressants from maternal blood, amniotic fluid, umbilical cord blood and/or breast milk. Penetration ratios were calculated by dividing the concentrations in amniotic fluid, umbilical cord plasma or breast milk by the maternal plasma concentration. When data from multiple studies were available, we calculated combined penetration ratios, weighting the study mean by study size.

  • Drug exposure during pregnancy: Current understanding and approaches to measure maternal-fetal drug exposure

    2023, Frontiers in Pharmacology
    Prescription drug use is prevalent during pregnancy, yet there is limited knowledge about maternal-fetal safety and efficacy of this drug use because pregnant individuals have historically been excluded from clinical trials. Approaches to study fetal drug pharmacology are limited and must be evaluated for feasibility and accuracy.
