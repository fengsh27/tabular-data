Summary

What is known and objective

Depression during pregnancy is common and includes risks for mother and child. Pharmacokinetics of venlafaxine may be changed during pregnancy. This study aimed to describe changes in metabolic ratios and concentrations of venlafaxine and its main metabolite O-desmethylvenlafaxine during and after pregnancy.

Methods

To study this, we used data from our study of compliance to Antidepressants During Pregnancy (the ADAP study) to investigate the course of venlafaxine and O-desmethylvenlafaxine concentrations during pregnancy and in the period post-partum.

Results and discussion

We found that the venlafaxine concentration significantly changed during pregnancy when compared to the post-partum period (P = 0·028). The median concentration of venlafaxine in the first trimester was 98·9% (54·2–292·0%), the second 100·0% (46·5–264·0%) and the third trimester 87·0% (61·5–217·2%). We did not found differences in O-desmethylvenlafaxine concentrations in the different trimesters of pregnancy compared with the post-partum period, P = 0·565. Also the ratio of O-desmethylvenlafaxine/venlafaxine concentrations increased significantly from 76·9% (range 32·8–142·0%) in the first trimester to 196·7% (range 83·3–427·6%) in the third trimester compared with the post-partum period, P = 0·004. Further, three of seven patients had concentrations below the therapeutic reference range (100–400 μg/L) in any period of pregnancy, whereas no one had subtherapeutic concentrations in the post-partum period.

What is new and conclusion

Venlafaxine concentrations decreases during pregnancy, and the ratio of the concentrations of O-desmethylvenlafaxine/venlafaxine increases during pregnancy. Pregnant women using venlafaxine are at risk for subtherapeutic concentrations, therefore routine monitoring of concentrations venlafaxine and O-desmethylvenlafaxine is recommendable during pregnancy.

What is known and Objective

Depression and anxiety are widespread diseases in the general population, and often, comorbid psychiatric conditions occur. The lifetime prevalence of major depressive disorder (MDD) in the USA is about 16·2%.1 About 72% of patients with a lifetime incidence of MDD also met the criteria for at least one of the other psychiatric conditions including 59% with anxiety disorder, 24% with substance abuse and 30% with impulse control disorder.1 Recent data from the Amsterdam region of The Netherlands show that 16% of pregnant women show mild depressive symptoms and 12% of pregnant women show major depressive symptoms.2

Venlafaxine is a bicyclic antidepressant and is categorized as a serotonin–norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor (SNRI). It works by blocking the monoamine transporter for the key neurotransmitters (serotonin and norepinephrine) affecting mood. In high doses, venlafaxine also weakly inhibits the reuptake of dopamine. It is extensively metabolized in the liver via the CYP2D6 isoenzyme to O-desmethylvenlafaxine, which is just as potent as the parent compound, meaning that the differences in metabolism between extensive and poor metabolizer are not clinically important in terms of efficacy. The primary route of excretion of venlafaxine and its metabolites is via the kidneys. For venlafaxine and its main metabolite, a relationship between plasma concentrations and the therapeutic effect and toxicity has been established. The therapeutic reference range (TRR) is between 100 and 400 μg/L, as the sum of concentrations of both venlafaxine and O-desmethylvenlafaxine, the main metabolite.3

In general, during pregnancy, the volume of distribution increases (increase in plasma volume, total body water, increased amount of body fat), and lower concentration of drug-binding proteins is observed, as well as an increase in hepatic, renal and cardiac blood flow, and in the end increase in metabolism (induction by progesterone and oestrogen of metabolising enzymes).4, 5 This may also have an effect on plasma concentrations of tricyclic antidepressants (TCA). This may be exemplified by nortriptyline, where Wisner et al.6 showed that the dose of nortriptyline to prevent relapse was 1·6-fold elevated, when the preconception period was compared with the third trimester; however, the number of patients was small (n = 6).

So far, the course of the concentration of venlafaxine in subsequent trimesters during pregnancy is not known. However, one case report with inconclusive results was published by Klier et al. where elimination half-life of venlafaxine was shortened by a factor 2 when the third trimester was compared to the post-partum period. However, in the same patient, the AUC decreased by factor 3 when post-partum was compared to the third trimester.7 This highly suggests that not only venlafaxine metabolism was changed during pregnancy, but also parameters of distribution, absorption may have been changed. In this case series, we demonstrate that metabolism of venlafaxine is affected during pregnancy.

Methods

Setting

This observational study was performed in Zwolle, The Netherlands, at the Isala Clinics, a large 1000 beds secondary teaching hospital with about 3000 deliveries each year. We used the data from our study where we investigated the compliance of the use of antidepressants during pregnancy, the ADAP trial (NL 27726·075·09). This trial was approved by the hospital's medical ethics committee and the central medical ethics committee of The Netherlands (CCMO) and recently published.8 Once included and with a signed informed consent, patient's GP and pharmacist were informed that his/her patient participated in this trial to prevent the patient from discontinuation in medication information. The study protocol follows the (draft) guidelines of the USA. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) on pharmacokinetic studies in pregnancy.9

Inclusion and study scheme

Inclusion criteria for this study were written informed consent for the study protocol and current use of venlafaxine with the expectation of using this drug during the whole pregnancy. We excluded women who were mental incapable, had twin pregnancies, used drugs of abuse (not nicotine or alcohol), used additional other antidepressants or antipsychotic drugs, used drugs with the same or higher teratogenic risk classification, used any additional drugs with any influence on venlafaxine metabolism or had a compliance of pharmacotherapy <80% of the doses on right time. Another exclusion criterion was venlafaxine doses above 450 mg/day, to maintain linear pharmacokinetics of venlafaxine according to the data of the manufacturer. Each trimester blood was withdrawn to check the actual blood concentration, and further data about dosing schedule, demographic data (only at inclusion) and body weight were only noted at inclusion.

Dosing and sampling

Because the study was performed outside the hospital in a naturalistic setting, we used MEMS packages to be sure about dosing times (MEMS-6 track cap®, Aardex Ltd, Sion, Switzerland). Sampling times, through concentrations, were recorded electronically when blood was withdrawn. With these measures, we were able to record the time after dose (TAD) accurate and easily. TAD had to be constant (trough concentrations) during the study period, otherwise absolute concentrations cannot be compared. Samples were stored at −80 °C until analysis.

Blood concentration analysis

Plasma samples were analysed with a HPLC fluorescence detection (Jasco FP1520 fluorescence detector (excitation wavelength of 270 nm, and emission wavelength of 300 nm), Thermo AS3000 autosampler (Thermo scientific, Breda, The Netherlands), Thermo P1000 pump (Thermo scientific), Inertsil ODS-3V 5 mm 3·0 × 150 mm column (ATAS international, Eindhoven, The Netherlands), oven temperature of 30 °C). Samples were obtained from whole blood with disodium edetate as anticoagulant, centrifuged and further processed, following 200 μL plasma (the supernatant) added to 100 μL internal standard solution (metoprolol CRS 0·5 mg/L in water for injection), 200 μL pH 10·5 buffer solution and 5 mL butyl-methyl-ether (BME) (Merck, Darmstadt, Germany) mixed thoroughly for 10 min. Then, the mixture was centrifuged at 4 g, and the BME layer was transferred and evaporated until dry. Then, 200 μL eluents was added and vortexed for 20 s. Injection volume was 20 μL. Samples were eluted in 0·05 m KH2PO4 (pH 4·0)/acetonitril (75 : 20 (L/L)) with 1·5 mL/min.

The linearity of our developed method was between 50 and 1000 μg/L for both venlafaxine and O-desmethylvenlafaxine, which seems suitable for the expected plasma concentration range (100–400 μg/L). Within-day variations for 3 concentrations (75, 150 and 225 μg/L) and between-day variations for 3 concentrations (75, 150 and 225 μg/L) were lower than 5·9%, and the limit of quantification was 50 μg/L for both components. All reagents were of analytical grade. Acetonitril (UV grade) and methanol were purchased from Labscan® (Cuijck, The Netherlands), metoprolol was purchased from LGC (Wessel, Germany), and formic acid was purchased from Sigma-Aldrich Chemicals (Zwijndrecht, The Netherlands).

Analytical results below the lower limit of quantification (LLOQ) were replaced by the value of 0·5 × LLOQ (= 25 μg/L). We corrected the concentrations for dose changes. We assumed that the situation 3 months after delivery reflects the situation before pregnancy. All venlafaxine and O-desmethylvenlafaxine concentrations were calculated as a percentage of the concentration 3 months after delivery.

Statistics

We used the Friedman test (nonparametric, repeated measurements, P < 0·05 for statistical significance) for differences in the MR's and median concentration of venlafaxine and O-desmethylvenlafaxine between the trimesters and post-partum.

Results and Discussion

Seven of ten women using venlafaxine of the ADAP trial could be included. Two women were no samples at all (did not show up for blood withdrawal), and from 1 woman, no blood concentration at the post-partum period was known, due to loss of follow-up. The mean age was 30 ± 4 years, and no women used concomitant medication, cigarettes or alcohol. At start of the study (first trimester), the mean weight was 76 ± 15 kg and the mean height was 168 ± 6 cm. From the data from the MEMS packages, it was shown that 86% of the included women took the prescribed medication in more than 80% at the right time, so we calculated the results as all women were compliant. The highest dose of venlafaxine used was 225 mg in this population, so in all cases, linear pharmacokinetics can be assumed.

The median concentration of venlafaxine was 98·9% (range 54·2–292·0%) in the first trimester compared with the period post-partum and decreased to 87·0% (range 61.5–217.2%) in the third trimester (P = 0·028), see for details Table 1. For O-desmethylvenlafaxine, no significant decrease was found in concentration between the trimesters and the period post-partum (P = 0·565), see Fig. 1. The metabolic ratio of the concentrations venlafaxine and O-desmethylvenlafaxine changed significantly during pregnancy, P = 0·004. The median metabolic ratio of the first trimester was 76·9% (range 32·8–142·0%) compared with the post-partum period. In the second trimester, we found a metabolic ratio of 116·0% (range 24·0–177·0) and in the third trimester 196·7% (range 83·3–427·6%), see also Fig. 1 and Table 1 for details.

In the first trimester, three women had summed concentrations of venlafaxine and O-desmethylvenlafaxine below the TRR; in the second trimester, one woman had concentrations below TRR, and in the third trimester, two women had concentrations below the TRR, see also Table 1.

We found a statistical significant decrease (98·9% → 87·0%, P = 0·028) of the venlafaxine concentration during pregnancy as compared to the period post-partum. The metabolic ratio O-desmethylvenlafaxine/venlafaxine increased (76·9% → 196·7%, P = 0·018) during pregnancy as compared to the period post-partum. 6–8 weeks after the delivery, CYP2D6 metabolism returned to the state as before pregnancy.10 Therefore, we could use the concentrations data in the period 2–3 months after pregnancy to compare the metabolic ratio in the different trimesters of pregnancy. Our results are in line with previous published studies, where also a decrease in parent plasma concentrations was found in CYP2D6 metabolized antidepressants such as paroxetine, duloxetine, fluoxetine and citalopram.11 In general, it seems that the activity of CYP2D6 is increased during the course of pregnancy,10 which may be explained by early foetal CYP2D6 enzymes, which are present from the first trimester.12 However, in our cohort, we found only a decrease in the metabolite concentration in the third trimester of pregnancy as compared to the post-partum period, where Tracy et al.10 found increased metabolism in all trimesters of pregnancy. The small sample size could not be the only reason for the high interindividual variations in plasma concentrations found, as we know different genotypes of the CYP2D6 lead to different plasma concentrations. All patients had a metabolic ratio between 1 and 10, suggesting that these can be classified as extensive metabolizers.13 High interindividual variations irrespective of CYP2D6 status were also found in a large study in non-pregnant women and men in a naturalistic setting in Sweden.14

The main limitation of our study is the small sample size, and sometimes low concentrations of venlafaxine, where relatively a large number of patients had concentrations below the LLOQ. It is therefore hard to draw firm conclusions about the changes in metabolism of venlafaxine during pregnancy; however, we reached statistical significance.

We assumed linear pharmacokinetics of venlafaxine during pregnancy. Because increased activity of CYP2D6 seems to occur during pregnancy, zero order pharmacokinetics is not to be expected. The decrease in plasma concentration of venlafaxine may also be related to a decrease in bioavailability or increased volume of distribution. However, the increase in MR suggested an increased metabolism of venlafaxine, not excluding changes in other parameters. The unchanged O-desmethylvenlafaxine concentrations during pregnancy suggested that renal elimination is of first order and is not affected by pregnancy.

Another explanation for the decrease in venlafaxine concentration can be increased activity of the P-glycoprotein (P-gp) transporter. Venlafaxine is a substrate of P-gp. Herbert et al. found changes in the activity of P-gp transport in the third trimester of pregnancy resulting in increased clearance of digoxin. This was only a pivotal study, more evidence for this mechanism for venlafaxine is needed.15

The sum of concentrations of venlafaxine and O-desmethylvenlafaxine determines the clinical effect. We showed that two women, who were compliant to venlafaxine therapy, had concentrations below the TRR of 100–400 μg/L in the third trimester of pregnancy.3 Those women might be at risk for therapeutic failure during pregnancy where both women and the unborn child are at risk. However, it could be that the TRR of venlafaxine and its metabolite is not equal during pregnancy as compared to the normal population. Until then, it is important to monitor venlafaxine concentrations during pregnancy to prevent women from relapse of depression due to subtherapeutic concentrations.

What is new and Conclusion

The data from this small study suggest that there may be a decrease in venlafaxine concentrations during pregnancy, and the ratio of the concentrations of O-desmethylvenlafaxine/venlafaxine increases during pregnancy. Pregnant women using venlafaxine are at risk for subtherapeutic concentrations; therefore, routine monitoring of concentrations venlafaxine and O-desmethylvenlafaxine is recommendable during pregnancy.
