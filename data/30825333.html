Abstract

Background

Although few studies have used ketamine for induction and maintenance of pediatric anesthesia, official dosage recommendations are lacking. This study evaluates the outcomes of adult anesthetic doses in a pediatric population through pharmacokinetic modeling and computer simulations in an attempt to recommend an adequate ketamine dosing regimen.

Methods

Ketamine plasma concentration-time data in 19 children (age 8 months to 16 years; weight 5.5 to 67 kg) were analyzed according to a non-compartmental pharmacokinetic approach. The relationship between pharmacokinetic parameters and demographic covariates was mathematically characterized. A one-compartment open model was implemented to simulate the plasma profile following administration of 1–4.5 mg/kg IV bolus dose and 0.1–0.5 mg/kg/min continuous infusion of ketamine and to predict anesthesia onset and offset.

Key Results

Pharmacokinetic parameters determined were clearance 0.025 ± 0.008 L/kg/min; distribution volume 3.3 ± 1.3 L/kg; half-life 2.6 ± 1 h; and mean residence time 2.3 ± 0.64 h. Body weight was the best predictor of clearance and distribution volume according to a 0.75-power model. Using weight to scale doses was associated with limited variability in simulated concentrations. Ketamine administered as 2.25 mg/kg IV bolus dose, followed by 0.1 mg/kg/min continuous IV infusion enables anesthesia initiation within 3 minutes and maintains it for 3 hours.

Conclusions & Inferences

Weight-based dosing minimizes age-dependent variation in the plasma concentration of ketamine. Low-to-intermediate adult doses are suitable for induction and maintenance of safe anesthesia in children undergoing short-term surgical operations. However, this finding requires validation in controlled clinical trials before it is adopted into surgical standard practices.

Ketamine is a phencyclidine derivative, which functions primarily as an antagonist of the N-methyl-d-aspartate (NMDA) receptor. Ketamine has sedative properties in low doses and anesthetic properties in high doses. Also in sub-anesthetic doses, ketamine treats depression.1 Ketamine essentially acts on glutamate binding sites (NMDA) and non-NMDA receptors. The block of NMDA receptor is responsible for the specific ketamine amnesic and psychosensory effects, as well as the analgesia and neuroprotective properties.2

Ketamine has a low oral bioavailability.3 After systemic absorption, ketamine is rapidly distributed to the brain and other well–perfused tissues. Plasma protein binding of ketamine is low (10–30%).4 As a lipophilic molecule ketamine has a large steady-state volume of distribution (160–550 L/70 kg). Ketamine undergoes oxidative metabolism in the liver, mainly to norketamine by cytochrome P450 (CYP3A and CYP2B6 enzymes).5 The short half-life of ketamine in humans (2–4 hrs) results in rapid recovery after IV-induced anesthesia. Systemic clearance standardized to a 70-kg person is 60–147 L/hour and is equal to liver blood flow.6 Diminished hepatic flow in elderly individuals or patients with hepatic cirrhosis decreases the clearance of ketamine and increases its oral bioavailability.7

In children 4–10 years old, it was found8 that the body weight–normalized clearance (16.8 ml/kg/min) and distribution volume (1.9 L/kg) of ketamine were not significantly different from adult values after IV administration of 2 mg/kg and IM administration of 6 mg/kg doses. Another group9 reported slightly larger values, 22 ml/kg/minute and 2.8 L/kg for clearance and volume, respectively, when 3 mg/kg ketamine was injected intravenously in 2–8 year old children. Newborns and infants (1 week–30 months) experienced slightly slower clearance (15.7 ml/kg/min) compared to older children.10 Immaturity of the hepatic biotransformation capacity and kidney excretion potential in children younger than 1 year old11-13 may explain these observations.

Population pharmacokinetics of ketamine in children had been reported. In children 8.15 ± 3.5 years of age presenting for emergency care, elimination clearance and steady state distribution volume standardized to a 70-kg person were 90 L/hour/70 kg and 140.7 L/70 kg, respectively, following IV administration of 1–1.5 mg/kg racemic ketamine.14 Similar values were reported15 when the population pharmacokinetic data were pooled to racemic ketamine plasma concentrations collected in children with burns. A recent study16 reported similar estimates in 0.02- to 17.6-year-old children after the administration of a racemic mixture via IV and IM routes (elimination clearance, 38.9 L/hour/70 kg; steady-state distribution volume 184.8 L/70 kg). (S)-Ketamine administration as a 3.6 mg/kg/hour intravenous infusion in 0.02- to 12.5-year-old children under intensive care16 showed faster clearance (112 L/hr/70 kg) and larger volume (552 L/70 kg) compared to the racemic mixture.

Ketamine should not be administered to patients with untreated or uncontrolled hypertension or heart disease. Adverse events include increased cardiac output, tachycardia, seizures, visual hallucination, vivid dreams, panic attacks, aggressive behavior, respiratory depression, and cardiac arrest.17-19

For anesthesia in adults, ketamine is administered as a 1–4.5 mg/kg IV bolus dose followed by a 0.1–0.5 mg/minute/kg IV infusion. Intramuscular administration is also adequate for anesthesia induction at doses of 6.5–13 mg/kg.20 Ketamine is not approved in the United States for children younger than 16 years of age,20 although off-label use for sedation/analgesia has been described.21 Intravenous doses up to 2 mg/kg and additional intermittent doses are indicated based on need.21

Although few studies have examined the use of ketamine for induction and maintenance of pediatrics anesthesia,8, 22 official dosage recommendations for this indication have yet to surface. Therefore, the main goal of this study was to evaluate the performance of adult anesthetic doses in a pediatric population through pharmacokinetic modeling and computer simulations, in an attempt to recommend an adequate ketamine dosing regimen in this vulnerable population.

Pharmacokinetic Analysis

Ketamine plasma concentration-time data were analyzed individually using a pharmacokinetic noncompartmental approach. The linear trapezoidal rule was used to calculate the area under curve and area under moment curve from zero to the last observation time. The elimination rate constant of ketamine was estimated from the slope of the terminal portion of the curve after logarithmic transformation of the concentrations. Identification of the terminal portion was based on an iterative algorithm in which the last three, four, five, and so on points were regressed and the correlation coefficient was computed in each case. The slope associated with the largest coefficient was selected.

Pharmacokinetic Simulations and Ketamine Dose Optimization

According to Figure 5, the maximum anesthesia period achieved with adult doses of ketamine was approximately 3.5 hours, and it was associated with low-dose administration (1 mg/kg IV bolus dose +0.1 mg/kg/min IV infusion). Intermediate (2.5 mg/kg IV bolus dose +0.3 mg/kg/min IV infusion) and large doses (4.5 mg/kg IV bolus dose +0.5 mg/kg/min IV infusion) produce safe anesthesia for less than 30 and 20 minutes, respectively. Continuation of either dose for longer periods is expected to produce systemic toxicity.

For induction and maintenance of anesthesia in children, ketamine can be administered as 1–2.25 mg/kg IV bolus dose, followed by a 0.1–0.2 mg/kg/minute continuous IV infusion (Figures 6 and 7). This dosage regimen enables safe anesthesia initiation within 2–17 minutes (Figure 6) and maintains it for 1–3 hours (Figure 7). Based on Figure 6, anesthesia induction period decreases as the bolus dose and infusion rate levels increase. On the other hand, the period of safe anesthesia is not affected by the bolus dose level and decreased by increasing the infusion rate level (Figure 7).

Discussion

In the literature, average ketamine clearance and distribution volume reported for children were 0.016–0.022 L/kg/minute, and 1.9–3.7 L/kg, respectively.8-10 Accordingly, the body-weight normalized parameters computed in this study are in close agreement with the literature values.

The mean distribution volume of ketamine at steady state exceeded 50 L suggesting that the drug has extensive tissue distribution. The large distribution volume of ketamine is attributed to its inherent lipophilicity and poor plasma protein binding.24

The average total body clearance per kg of ketamine approximated hepatic blood flow as reported in the literature (0.018 L/kg/min),25 indicating perfusion rate-limited clearance. The high clearance rate precludes the possibility of drug accumulation upon repeated administration.

The increase in average total body clearance and steady state distribution volume with body weight is attributable to the maturation of the CYP3A4 enzyme11-13 (the enzyme responsible for the metabolism of ketamine), increased hepatic blood flow,26 and altered body composition with aging (e.g., percentage of body fat and fat mass index increase with age27).

Body weight power models with exponents of 0.75 and 1.0 have been proposed as a paradigm to describe the effect of growth on clearance and distribution volume, respectively.28 Thus, such models were evaluated in this study.

Limited sensitivity of plasma-concentration time behavior to body weight variation observed in this study is attributed to adjusting doses, rates and parameters by weight leading to insignificant variation in the dose-to-volume, rate-to-clearance, and clearance-to-volume ratios with weight. For example, in a 5-kg person, the ratios are 0.5, 10, 0.01, respectively, while in a 70-kg person, the ratios are 0.9, 18, 0.01, respectively, assuming a 2 mg/kg dose and a 0.3 mg/kg/minute rate. Although weight increased > 12 times, two of the aforementioned ratios did not increase > 2 times, and the third did not change at all. Therefore, it is reasonable to conclude that using body weight to scale doses/rates is associated with limited variability in the expected outcomes of ketamine administration. Thus, it appears that stratifying dosage regimen by age is not necessary.

Although several studies have investigated the pharmacokinetic properties of ketamine in children,14, 15, 23 none of these studies evaluated adult anesthetic doses in real situations or via computer modeling. Moreover, no study has recommended a simple dosage regimen for anesthesia induction and maintenance that can be easily implemented by clinicians.

Pharmacokinetic/pharmacodynamics modeling and simulations are powerful tools that allow selection of doses to be tested in clinical trials and setting the safety limits for dosing. This issue is critical in vulnerable populations, such as children, where ethical concerns are always raised, recruiting participants is quite difficult, evaluation of a large number of doses is not acceptable, complicated and lengthy review processes are demanded by regulatory agencies, and testing unapproved dosage regimens is associated with tremendous risks, especially with drugs known to have severe side effects. It is noteworthy that submissions to regulatory agencies supported with pharmacokinetic modeling and simulation studies have become a necessity to facilitate and expedite decision-making during regulatory review.29

The pharmacokinetic/pharmacodynamics modeling and simulations approach made it possible to explore the anticipated safe anesthesia induction and maintenance periods of ketamine subsequent to adult dose administration. Our model predicts that 2.25 mg/kg IV bolus dose, followed by 0.1 mg/kg/minute continuous IV infusion is capable of initiating anesthesia within 3 minutes and terminating it in not less than 3 hours after administration. Loading with 1 mg/kg IV bolus dose instead, fails to initiate anesthesia in less than 15 minutes. Intravenous injection of 2–3 mg/kg ketamine in children was previously reported.8, 9

A limitation of this study is the wide age range of the participants and the inability to recruit many children younger than 1 year old. This drawback may have influenced our judgment regarding the necessity of ketamine dose stratification by age. Moreover, hepatic and renal function test results were not available in this study. It would have been interesting to evaluate the effect of physiological factor on the pharmacokinetic parameters of ketamine. In this study, mechanistic pathways behind the influence of growth on clearance and distribution volume were not elucidated in order to avoid complex physiological and biochemical experiments and maintain the clinical merit of the work.

Conclusions

Weight-based dosing minimizes age-dependent variation in the plasma concentration of ketamine. Low-to-intermediate adult doses are suitable for induction and maintenance of safe anesthesia in children undergoing short-term surgical operations. This finding requires validation in a controlled clinical trial before it is adopted into surgical standard practices.
