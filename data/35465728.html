ABSTRACT

Vancomycin dosing used in neonates results frequently in insufficient concentrations. A vancomycin dose-optimization protocol consisting of an individualization of loading and maintenance doses (administered during continuous infusion) through a previously validated pharmacokinetic model was implemented in our center. This monocenter retrospective study aimed to compare vancomycin average concentration (Cavg) in the therapeutic range (15 to 25 mg/L) and biological and clinical parameters before and after implementation of this protocol. A total of 60 and 59 courses of vancomycin treatment in 45 and 49 patients were analyzed in groups before and after implementation, respectively. Initial vancomycin Cavg were more frequently in the therapeutic range in the group after implementation (74.6% versus 28.3%, P < 0.001), with 1.6-fold higher Cavg (20.3 [17.0–22.2] mg/L versus 12.9 [11.3–17.0] mg/L, P < 0.001). Considering all Cavg during longitudinal therapeutic drug monitoring (TDM), the frequency of therapeutic Cavg was higher in the group after implementation (74.8% [n = 103] versus 31% [n = 116], P < 0.001). The dose optimization protocol was also associated with a reduced time to obtain a negative blood culture (P < 0.001) and fewer antibiotic switches (P = 0.025), without increasing the frequency of nephrotoxicity. Clinical outcomes also appeared to be improved, with less periventricular leukomalacia (P = 0.021), trended toward less respiratory instability (P = 0.15) and a shorter duration of vasoactive drug use (P = 0.18) for neonates receiving personalized doses of vancomycin. This personalized vancomycin dose protocol improves vancomycin exposure in neonates, with good safety, and suggests an improvement in biological and clinical outcomes.

KEYWORDS: vancomycin, neonates, pharmacokinetics, therapeutic drug monitoring, model-informed precision dosing

INTRODUCTION

Neonates admitted to neonatal intensive care units are at increased risk of sepsis due to their immature immune function and the invasive care required, and it is still a source of morbidity and mortality. Vancomycin, a glycopeptide with bactericidal activity, is the first-line antibiotic for the treatment of such nosocomial infections, most often due to coagulase-negative staphylococci (1–3). However, this antibiotic exhibits high pharmacokinetic variability, especially in neonates, and a narrow therapeutic range, justifying therapeutic drug monitoring (TDM). Vancomycin plasma concentrations are very often insufficient in neonates, with proportions of subtherapeutic concentrations varying from 47 to 93%, depending on the study (4–8). In this context, approaches for vancomycin dose adjustment for neonates, most frequently based on pharmacokinetic models, have been proposed (4, 9–14). However, few such strategies have been implemented in routine care and/or validated by clinical studies (15, 16), and the impact of these strategies on the clinical-biological evolution of neonates has never been evaluated.

The very frequent subtherapeutic vancomycin concentrations measured during continuous infusion (average concentration, Cavg) observed in the neonatal intensive care unit of the Grenoble Alpes University Hospital led to the implementation of a vancomycin dose-optimization protocol in routine care (4). This change in practice motivated us to carry out a retrospective study, the primary objective of which was to compare the proportion of initial vancomycin Cavg in the therapeutic range of 15 to 25 mg/L before and after the implementation of this protocol. In addition, we also investigated the impact of this change in practice on biological parameters and clinical outcomes.

(i) Safety of the protocol. Only one patient showed nephrotoxicity in the before group, and two in the after group, which was not statistically different. The highest Cavg obtained was 12.2 mg/L for the unique patient who developed renal dysfunction in the before group and 72.8 mg/L and 25.4 mg/L for the two patients of the after group. These three patients were also treated with gentamicin, and two (one in the group before, one in the group after) developed anuria immediately after surgical procedures (one emergency surgery for severe acute enterocolitis and one surgery for myelomeningocele).

(ii) Biological parameters. The after group showed a shorter time to negative blood culture (P < 0.001), even though blood culture frequencies were similar in the two groups (data not shown). Furthermore, the biological inflammatory syndrome decreased more frequently, with fewer relapses, in the after group, although these findings did not reach significance.

(iii) Antibiotic therapy. Antibiotic switches were significantly less frequent for the after group. The duration of vancomycin therapy also tended to be lower. However, we did not observe this tendency when considering only documented infections due to Gram-positive cocci (before (n = 28): 10.0 [7.75–12.0] days versus after (n = 19): 9.0 [8.0–10.5] days, P = 0.425).

(iv) Immediate clinical outcomes. The proportion of infants requiring increased ventilatory support, as well as the duration of vasoactive drug use, was smaller for the after group, without reaching statistical significance. There were no differences in terms of the duration of parenteral nutrition, insulin therapy, or the number of catheter removals between the two groups.

(v) Neonatal morbidity. There were significantly fewer cases of periventricular leukomalacia and a tendency toward fewer cases of severe bronchopulmonary dysplasia in the after group. There were no differences in the length of hospitalization.

DISCUSSION

This study is the first to demonstrate the utility of model-informed precision dosing of vancomycin administered by continuous infusion in neonates in terms of improved vancomycin exposure and clinical and biological outcomes in clinical practice.

First, vancomycin exposure in the first hours was improved with the vancomycin dose optimization protocol, with a 2.6-fold increase in the proportion of initial Cavg in the therapeutic range. Before implementation of the dose-optimization protocol, only 28.3% of initial vancomycin Cavg were therapeutic, in accordance with results reported in previous studies using various standard dosing regimens (from 25.1 to 41.4%) (4, 5, 7, 8, 17). The a priori vancomycin dose adjustment allowed us to reach a proportion of 74.6% of initial plasma therapeutic Cavg (measured during vancomycin continuous infusion), very close to the 70.1 and 72% reported in studies using the same pharmacokinetic model and therapeutic range (4, 15). This finding is all the more in favor of adequate vancomycin exposure in the first hours of treatment with the dose optimization protocol that initial vancomycin Cavg were determined earlier in the group treated under it (i.e., after group) than the group that was not (i.e., before group). The increase in initial vancomycin therapeutic Cavg resulted from an increase in loading and maintenance doses of vancomycin with the dose optimization protocol, these increases being particularly high in extremely preterm neonates (who are the most at risk of infection-induced complications).

Although vancomycin exposure correlates with therapeutic efficacy, it is also necessary to take into account the individual sensitivity of each bacterial strain (18). We thus evaluated initial AUC/MIC and Cavg/MIC ratios for documented infections and found them to more often reach the recommended thresholds (19, 20) in the after than the before group. In addition, the vancomycin dose-optimization protocol resulted in an improvement of vancomycin exposure throughout the duration of treatment, with an increase in the proportion of therapeutic Cavg during longitudinal TDM from 31 to 74.8%.

The increase in vancomycin Cavg resulting from the implementation of the dose-optimization protocol did not change the frequency of nephrotoxicity, which was very low for both the before (1/59) and after (2/57) groups. In addition, all neonates that developed nephrotoxicity had at least one additional risk factor of renal function deterioration (aminoside cotreatment and/or surgical procedure). Such findings are in accordance with those of the study of Leroux et al. (15) and further supports the safety of the vancomycin dose-optimization protocol.

In addition, the improvement of vancomycin exposure was accompanied by an improvement in several biological and microbiological outcomes, with for example, a shorter time to obtaining negative blood cultures and a reduction in the number of antibiotic switches. The reduced duration of bacteremia in neonates with the a priori vancomycin dose adjustment is in accordance with the results of recent studies showing that insufficient vancomycin exposure in the first 48 to 72 h is associated with prolonged bacteremia in children (21, 22). The clinical response to vancomycin therapy of adult patients with lower respiratory tract infections has also been shown to be better for patients with high vancomycin exposure and rapid bacterial eradication (23), but similar data for late-onset sepsis in neonates are still lacking. Here, we found that neonates benefiting from the vancomycin dose-optimization protocol tended to have less respiratory instability, a reduced duration of vasoactive drug use, and a greater decrease in inflammatory syndrome during the first 24 to 48 h of therapy, as well as fewer relapses. Although significance was not reached, such tendencies concerning clinical and biological outcomes have never been reported for neonates. A recent prospective comparative study evaluating the impact of an early Bayesian dose-adjustment protocol of vancomycin in children, for example, did not find any differences in terms of the time to obtain apyrexia or the evolution of C reactive protein (CRP) values (13). However, our results are in accordance with those of the retrospective study of Cardile et al., which showed that optimization of vancomycin exposure in adults through TDM was associated with significant clinical improvement relative to patients without TDM (24).

Late-onset sepsis has been identified as a risk factor for neonatal morbidity (25–31). We thus investigated the impact of vancomycin dose optimization on such complications, which has not been studied to date. We found fewer cases of periventricular leukomalacia and a trend toward fewer cases of severe bronchopulmonary dysplasia in patients benefiting from the dose-optimization protocol, without any difference in terms of the length of hospital stay.

This study had several limitations. Aside from its retrospective design, we evaluated vancomycin exposure through Cavg, for which there is no consensus in terms of the therapeutic range (17, 32–34), whereas the unbound Cavg (35) (or unbound AUC and AUC/MIC) (12) would have been better parameters. In addition, interference leading to underestimation of the vancomycin concentration with IgM levels of >15 mg/L was reported for the PETINIA technique on the Vista device (36). As we used this method until June 2019, we cannot exclude that vancomycin Cavg for some neonates in the before group were underestimated due to such interference. However, IgM levels in neonates, especially premature infants, are low (37) and would have been unlikely to interfere with vancomycin quantification by PETINIA. Finally, although our findings concerning the clinical benefits of vancomycin dose optimization are very promising, they must be interpreted with caution. Indeed, both groups included neonates for whom the suspicion of sepsis was not confirmed by blood culture (47.5 and 60% of courses in the before and after groups, respectively). Moreover, concerning neonatal morbidities, all risk factors for periventricular leukomalacia (for example, intrauterine infection or early-onset sepsis) (38, 39) and broncho-pulmonary dysplasia (chorioamnionitis, Apgar score, surfactant, postnatal steroids, treatment for patent ductus arteriosus) (26, 29) were not taken into account.

Conclusion.

Our study demonstrates that the vancomycin dose-optimization protocol increases vancomycin exposure in neonates and is associated with a significant improvement in biological and clinical outcomes, with good safety in terms of nephrotoxicity. Further prospective studies conducted on larger populations are expected to confirm the clinical benefits of optimized dosing regimens for vancomycin in neonates (14), before their generalization in all neonatal units.

MATERIALS AND METHODS

Study design.

This comparative, retrospective, single-center study was conducted in the neonatal intensive care unit of the Grenoble Alpes University Hospital in France. A protocol of vancomycin dose optimization was implemented in routine care in September 2019. Neonates treated with vancomycin as a continuous infusion before or after its implementation between 1 August 2018, and 31 October 2020, were included in groups before or after implementation (see flow chart in Fig. 1). Vancomycin therapy was started in cases of suspicion of neonatal sepsis due to clinical instability and/or altered biological parameters. The duration of vancomycin therapy was left to the discretion of the clinician according to clinical evolution and the results of blood cultures.

Inclusion criteria were neonates treated with vancomycin with at least one determination of vancomycin Cavg during this period. Exclusion criteria were children for whom their legal representatives objected to the use of their medical data, vancomycin treatment initiated in another center, a nonstandard dose of vancomycin for meningitis in the before group, and the vancomycin dose-optimization protocol not used for the after group.

Biological (plasma creatinine and CRP levels, vancomycin Cavg, blood culture) and clinical (type of respiratory support, use of vasoactive drugs, parenteral nutrition and insulin therapy, catheter removal, presence of bronchopulmonary dysplasia and periventricular leukomalacia, and hospitalization length) data were retrospectively collected from medical records (see “Secondary Objectives,” below, for more details), while MIC of bacteria isolated from blood culture (and stored at room temperature) were measured specifically for research.

This noninterventional study involving data and samples from human participants was carried out according to the Declaration of Helsinki and current French regulations. The investigator (Elodie Gautier-Veyret, Pharm.D., Ph.D.) has signed a commitment to comply with Reference Methodology no. 004 issued by the French authorities Commission Nationale de l'Informatique et des Libertés (CNIL). Participants were all informed and did not object. Written consent for participation was not required for this study, in accordance with national legislation and institutional requirements.

The raw data supporting the conclusions of this study will be made available by the authors according to General Data Protection Regulations, without undue reservation. Ethical approval for the study was obtained on 1 September 2021 (CECIC Rhone-Alpes-Auvergne, Clermont-Ferrand, Institutional Review Board [IRB] 5891).

Vancomycin dose optimization protocol.

The protocol of vancomycin dose optimization consists of the individualization of initial vancomycin doses (both loading and maintenance doses) through a previously validated pharmacokinetic model (4) and further dose adjustment following TDM, when needed. Initial doses were calculated by neonatologists using an Excel file (see Fig. S1), with formulas previously proposed by Zhao et al. (4). These formulas derived from a one-compartment pharmacokinetic model are based on weights at birth and at treatment initiation, plasma creatinine level, and postnatal age (see Fig. S1 for details). The first vancomycin Cavg measurement was then performed at 24 h, with the therapeutic range for vancomycin Cavg being between 15 and 25 mg/L (4). Dose adjustments were proposed in the reports of the biological analyses in the event of out-of-range vancomycin Cavg (<15 mg/L or >25 mg/L). The target Cavg used for dose calculations was 20 mg/L.

The empirical vancomycin dosing regimen before the implementation of this protocol, based on the corrected gestational age (32), is described in Table S1. Before September 2019, the first vancomycin Cavg measurement was performed approximatively 48 h after vancomycin treatment initiation. A similar therapeutic range was recommended in our institution during this period, but no specific dose adjustment was recommended.

After the first vancomycin Cavg measurement, the frequency of the following vancomycin measurements (i.e., longitudinal TDM) was left to the discretion of the clinician for both periods (before and after implementation of the protocol of vancomycin dose optimization).

For both periods, loading doses were administered through a 1-h infusion followed immediately by continuous infusion of maintenance doses.

Vancomycin therapeutic drug monitoring.

Plasma vancomycin concentrations were measured at steady state in a clinical laboratory using a commercial immunometric assay during routine care. A Dimension Vista system (Siemens Healthcare Diagnostics, Saint Denis, France) device with a particle-enhanced turbidimetric inhibition immunoassay (PETINIA) was used until June 2019 (range of concentrations, 0.8 to 50 mg/L), from which time a chemiluminescence-based method on an Architect (Abbott laboratories, Rungis, France) system was used to quantify vancomycin (range of concentrations, 3 to 100 mg/L). Before this change, the correlation between these two methods was verified on 39 samples, with a good coefficient of correlation (r2 = 0.9942) and a slope near 1 (0.918). The within- and between-day accuracy and precision of both methods fulfilled Food and Drug Administration validation criteria.

Objectives.

(i) Main objective. The main objective of this study was to compare the proportion of initial vancomycin Cavg in the therapeutic range before and after the implementation of this protocol. The primary endpoint was the percentage of initial vancomycin Cavg in the therapeutic range.

(ii) Secondary objectives. The secondary objectives were to evaluate the impact of this vancomycin dose-optimization protocol on various biological and clinical outcomes assessed during routine care.

Vancomycin-induced nephrotoxicity, MICs, initial vancomycin Cavg/MIC and AUC/MIC ratios, duration of vancomycin therapy, number of antibiotic switches due to lack of efficacy of vancomycin (excluding antibiotic deescalation), duration of bacteremia, rate of relapse, evolution of biological inflammatory syndrome, and rate of catheter removal were assessed. In addition, classically unstable clinical parameters in sepsis (25–27, 40–42) (rate of increased respiratory support, duration of vasoactive drug use, parenteral nutrition, insulin therapy were all evaluated from vancomycin initiation), as well as neonatal morbidities associated with infections (25–31, 43) (bronchopulmonary dysplasia, periventricular leukomalacia, total duration of hospitalization until discharge) were studied.

Nephrotoxicity was defined as either a 2-fold increase or an increase of at least 0.6 mg/dL (or 52.8 μmol/L) of plasma creatinine concentration from baseline at treatment initiation until the end of vancomycin therapy (44). Creatinine concentrations were determined in a clinical laboratory, using an enzymatic method on an Atellica device (Siemens Healthcare Diagnostics, Saint Denis, France).

In the event of a documented bloodstream bacterial infection due to Gram-positive cocci, the MIC of vancomycin against bacteria isolated from blood cultures was measured by broth microdilution using vancomycin/teicoplanin MIC strips (UMIC; Biocentric, Bandol, France) in a clinical laboratory. Vancomycin Cavg/MIC and AUC/MIC ratios were calculated, with respective thresholds of ≥8 (5, 19) and 400 (20).

The duration of bacteremia was assessed by calculating the time between vancomycin initiation and the first negative blood culture. Relapses were defined as a recurrent positive blood culture with the same pathogen (same species with similar antibiogram) (45) within 7 days after obtaining a negative blood culture. The latter two criteria were only assessed in children with a positive blood culture.

Evolution of the biological inflammatory syndrome was assessed by the evolution of CRP within the first 24 to 48 h of treatment in patients with an initial CRP value of >10 mg/L. A decrease of inflammatory syndrome was defined as a decrease of at least 10 mg/L of the CRP value between the initial measure at the initiation of vancomycin and the following measure after 24 to 48 h of treatment.

The frequency of biological analysis (CRP measurement, blood culture and plasma creatinine measurement) was left to the discretion of the neonatologist.

The respiratory status was assessed by the increase in ventilatory support during vancomycin therapy, i.e., switching to continuous positive airway pressure, bi-level positive airway pressure, or mechanical ventilation and switching to high-frequency oscillatory ventilation for patients already intubated and ventilated. The presence of severe bronchopulmonary dysplasia and periventricular leukomalacia was recorded at the time of discharge from hospital according to criteria of the National Institute of Child Health workshop (46, 47) and the de Vries et al. grading system (48), respectively.

Statistical analysis.

The sample size required to show a doubling of the proportion of initial vancomycin therapeutic concentration with 80% power and a two-sided alpha risk of 0.05 was 59 courses of treatment for each group (assuming a proportion of 30% of initial vancomycin therapeutic Cavg). This calculation was performed using G*Power software (version 3.1.9.7; Heinrich-Heine-University, Germany). Data are reported as medians (25th to 75th percentiles) or n (%). Chi-square (or Fischer) and Mann-Whitney tests were used. A P value of 0.05 was considered statistically significant. All statistical analyses were performed using Jamovi software (version 1.2.27).
