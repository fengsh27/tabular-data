Summary

Background : It has been suggested that nasal administration of s-ketamine may be used to improve sedation or premedication in combination with nasal midazolam in pediatric patients. In this study we measured and compared plasma concentrations of s-ketamine and its main metabolite s-norketamine after nasal and i.v. administration in preschool children.

Methods : During sevoflurane anaesthesia, 20 children, aged 1–7 years, weight 11–25 kg, received s-ketamine 2 mg·kg−1 either intranasally (Group IN, n = 10), or i.v. (Group IV, n = 10). Six venous blood samples were obtained up to 60 min after drug administration for measurement of s-ketamine and s-norketamine plasma concentrations.

Results : Plasma concentrations [mean ± sd] of s-ketamine in group IN peaked at 355 ± 172 ng·ml−1 within 18 ± 13 min. vs. 1860 ± 883 ng·ml−1 within 3 ± 1 min. in group IV (P < 0.01). Plasma concentrations of s-norketamine in group IN peaked at 90 ± 128 ng·ml−1 within 50 ± 11 min. vs. 429 ± 277 ng·ml−1 within 40 ± 16 min. in group IV (P < 0.01). One child in group IN experienced rapid and high level s-ketamine absorption with a peak plasma concentration of 732 ng·ml−1 after 2 min., which decreased to 274 ng·ml−1 after 60 min. Systolic blood pressure and heart rate remained unaltered in both study groups after s-ketamine administration.

Conclusions : Nasal administration of s-ketamine 2 mg·kg−1 results in a wide spread of plasma concentrations and absorption times. Rapid and high level drug absorption after nasal drug administration is possible. The use of a pulse oximeter and continuous observation after nasal administration of s-ketamine for pediatric premedication is recommended.

Introduction

Racemic ketamine, an anesthetic drug also used for premedication in pediatric patients, is now being replaced by its enantiomer s-ketamine in many European countries. Compared with the racemate, s-ketamine provides approximately twice the anesthetic (1) and analgesic (2) effect. Furthermore, there are fewer psychomimetic side effects (3). Nasal administration of s-ketamine and midazolam has been shown to provide good induction conditions in preschool children (4). Whereas plasma concentrations of midazolam and racemic ketamine after i.v. and nasal administration in children have already been investigated (5,6), there are no data available concerning s-ketamine and its main metabolite s-norketamine.

In order to obtain data on the speed of drug absorption and resulting maximum concentrations we measured plasma concentrations of s-ketamine and s-norketamine in children after nasal and i.v. administration.

Methods

Following local ethics committee approval and written informed parental consent, we prospectively studied 20 children (aged 1 to 7 years), physical status ASA I and II, scheduled for minor elective dental or ophthalmological surgery. Exclusion criteria included: Rhinopharyngitis, known allergies to s-ketamine, midazolam or sevoflurane and any preoperative medication other than fluoride tablets. According to institutional standards, patients were premedicated with rectal midazolam 0.3 mg·kg−1 approximately 30 min before induction of anesthesia. All children had an inhalational induction with sevoflurane and N2O in 50% oxygen. To facilitate tracheal intubation cis-atracurium 0.1 mg·kg−1 was given into a peripheral i.v. catheter. After completion of the induction 10 patients (group IN) scheduled for dental and cleft palate surgery received preservative-free s-ketamine (Ketanest S, Parke Davis, Berlin, Germany) 2 mg·kg−1 (0.08 ml·kg−1) by injection into the nasal cavity. Another 10 patients (group IV) scheduled for strabismus repair received an intravenous bolus of s-ketamine 2 mg·kg−1, injected over 10 s. In group IV the i.v. catheter was flushed twice with 10 ml saline immediately after injection of s-ketamine. Anaesthesia was maintained with N2O in 30% O2 and sevoflurane as necessary. No additional systemic analgesics were given during the 60 min. study period. Only the patients in group i.v. (dental surgery) received additional regional anesthesia (bupivacaine 0.25%, 1–3 ml) injected by the surgeon. In both groups heart rate, pulse oximetry, concentrations of carbon dioxide, oxygen and sevoflurane were monitored continuously, and noninvasive arterial pressure was recorded at regular intervals during anesthesia.

Blood sampling and assay

Six venous blood samples (1.5 ml in EDTA tubes) were obtained from the i.v. catheter where s-ketamine was given 2, 5, 10, 20, 40, and 60 min after drug administration. Plasma was separated by centrifugation and stored at −20°C until analysis. Plasma samples for the measurement of s-ketamine levels were prepared according to a modification of a method described by Colin et al. (7). Quantification was achieved with a GC-MS (HP6890-MSD5973, Agilent, Waldbronn, Germany) equipped with a HP5-MS 30 m × 0.25 μm × 0.25 mm column (Agilent, Waldbronn, Germany) and a 10 m × 0.25 mm retention gap (Supelco, Deisenhofen, Germany) using sim-mode. The chromatographic condition was splitless injection of 0.5 μl extract, resolved in 25 μl toluene (VWR, Darmstadt, Germany) with an injector temperature at 265°C and an oven temperature of 95°C for 2 min. The temperature was then elevated at a rate of 80°C min−1 to 265 °C, kept for 10 min and then further heated to 310 °C at the same rate. The quality was controlled by extraction of plasma probes of known concentrations for s-ketamine (m/z = 180.0, RT = 7.76 min, 100 ng. 100 μl−1 plasma) with a recovery of 61% (n = 44). Intra (inter-) day coefficients of variation for s-ketamine were 4.2% (1.7%). Two internal standards, fentanyl (m/z = 245.2, RT = 12.67 min) and 3-methylfentanyl (m/z = 259.0 RT = 13.2 min) were used. The limits of detection were 1.70 ng·100 μl−1 plasma in a tested linearity range from 3 to 1500 ng·100 μl−1 plasma.

The maximum plasma concentration (Cmax) and the time to reach Cmax (TCmax) after drug administration were read directly from the concentration – versus – time data.

Statistics

One-way analysis of variance (anova) or the Kruskal-Wallis test were performed as appropriate for intra- and intergroup comparison of all continuous variables; when indicated, a Bonferroni's correction was performed for post hoc comparisons. Statistical analysis was performed using SPSS for Windows, version 11.0 (SPSS Inc., Chicago, IL, USA). Data are presented as mean ± sd unless otherwise mentioned. P values of <0.05 were considered statistically significant.

Compared with baseline values, obtained after induction of anesthesia immediately before administration of s-ketamine, heart rate (HR) and systolic blood pressure (SBP) remained stable within normal limits during the 60 min study period, without intergroup differences at any corresponding time point (see Figure 2).

Discussion

In pediatric patients nasally administered s-ketamine 2 mg·kg−1 induces a mean plasma concentration of 355 ± 172 ng·ml−1 with a mean TCmax.of 18 ± 13 min. These results are difficult to interpret because to our best knowledge there are no data available about the clinical effects of different s-ketamine plasma-concentrations in children. Our data in group IN are comparable with those published by Koinig et al. (8), who measured s-ketamine plasma concentrations in children after i.m. and caudal administration. In their study Cmax and TCmax after i.m. administration of s-ketamine 1 mg·kg−1 were 367 ± 131 ng·ml−1, and 11.2 ± 5.4 min, respectively. TCmax after nasal administration appears slower and more variable than after i.m. injection.

In this study nasal s-ketamine was administered after induction of anesthesia, with a child lying on the operation table absolutely motionless. This probably might have led to absorption rates which may slightly differ from those in awake children, where there are several physiological and pharmacological factors that can influence drug absorption and metabolism, such as anxiety, position during administration, i.e. supine and upright posture, drug(s) used concurrently, or haemodynamic changes. However, like Malinovsky et al. (5) we decided to perform a study with both drug administration and blood sampling under general anesthesia, because placement of an i.v.-catheter, required for subsequent venous blood-sampling starting at 2 min. after drug administration, may distress an awake child, regardless whether or not a sedative premedication was given and the skin was prepared with a local anesthetic.

Like Malinovsky et al. (5), who studied the pharmacokinetics of racemic ketamine in children after nasal, i.v. and rectal application, we found a wide range of individual plasma concentration profiles after both nasal and i.v. drug administration. One patient in Group IN experienced very rapid and high-level absorption via the nasal and pharyngeal mucosa, leading to a Cmax of 732 ng·ml−1 for s-ketamine only 2 min after drug administration and a Cmax of only 27 ng·ml−1 for s-norketamine, after 40 min, indicating a low degree of hepatic first pass metabolism. However, 60 min. later plasma concentrations of s-ketamine and s-norketamine had fallen to 274, and 22 ng·ml−1, respectively.

In case of an initial sedation failure after nasal administration of s-ketamine 2 mg·kg−1 as a premedication, questions about a second dose and possible side effects could arise. Our data showed much interindividual variation in absorptions. Thus the clinical effects of an early second dose of nasal s-ketamine in case of an initial sedation failure are unpredictable and we would therefore not recommend this strategy or at least suggest close monitoring of the patient.

S-norketamine, the main metabolite of s-ketamine may contribute to the analgesic effect of s-ketamine. Grant et al. (9) found an analgesic effect following oral ketamine 0.5 mg·kg−1 despite a very low plasma concentration of 40 ng·ml−1. In their study norketamine plasma concentrations after oral administration and i.m. injection of ketamine 0.5 mg·kg−1 were 200 ± 44 and 90 ± 10 ng·ml−1, respectively, and they concluded that norketamine also has analgesic properties with a threshold plasma concentration of 55 ng·ml−1. In our study the mean maximum plasma concentrations for s-norketamine in groups IN and IV were 90 ± 128 and 429 ± 277 ng·ml−1, respectively. There were patients in both study groups in whom the highest s-norketamine plasma concentrations were measured 60 min. after drug administration, so that we could not formally calculate mean Cmax and TCmax for s-norketamine.

Most data on ketamine and s-ketamine pharmacokinetics result from adult volunteer studies without comedication (9–11). Both benzodiazepines and ketamine are metabolized via the cytochrome P450 system. The enzymes catalysing the metabolism of s-ketamine have not been identified but there are indications of metabolic interaction between diazepam and ketamine (12). All patients in our study had received routine premedication with rectal midazolam 30 min before induction.

Measurements of s-ketamine and s-norketamine plasma concentrations after i.v. administration were performed to have data from a control group after using the route of administration, which is the gold standard. Not surprisingly, following i.v.-injection mean s-ketamine plasma concentrations were significantly higher than after nasal administration throughout the study period (see Figure 1). For s-norketamine mean plasma concentrations were significantly higher in group i.v. from 5 min after drug administration until the end of the study period. Intravenous s-ketamine 2 mg·kg−1 resulted in a Cmax of 1860 ± 883 ng·ml−1 after 3 ± 1 min., a value that has been reported to be associated with surgical anesthesia in adults (2). However, 60 min after drug administration plasma concentrations had fallen to values below the adult sedation threshold.

In summary we have seen a wide range of individual plasma concentration profiles and one particular case of rapid and high-level drug absorption after nasal administration of s-ketamine. Therefore we would recommend the use of a pulse oximeter and continuous observation after nasal drug administration for pediatric premedication.
